## Carousel Content: Before/After Format

Generate structured content for a multi-slide {{platform.name}} carousel PDF showing side-by-side transformations.

### Output Format (strict — follow exactly)

TITLE: [Frame the transformation]
SUBTITLE: [Optional one-line subtitle]
PAIR 1 BEFORE: [Weak version — in quotes if applicable]
PAIR 1 AFTER: [Strong rewrite or improvement]
PAIR 1 NOTE: [1 sentence explaining the principle behind the change]
PAIR 2 BEFORE: [Weak version]
PAIR 2 AFTER: [Strong rewrite]
PAIR 2 NOTE: [1 sentence explanation]
...continue for 4-6 pairs...
CTA: [Specific call-to-action related to the topic]

### Rules

- Exactly 4-6 pairs
- Before examples must be realistic — things your audience actually does
- After examples must show concrete improvement
- Contrast must be striking and educational
- Notes explain the principle, not just the change
- Frame as strategy, not just editing
- CTA must be specific to the topic

### Voice rules for slide format

This is not a text post. Every word must earn its place on the slide. Apply Signal Stance's voice with these before/after-specific adjustments:

1. **Senior-pro / fractional / founder calibration in both columns.** The "before" should make a Director / VP / fractional reader wince with recognition — not material that's clearly aimed at entry-level professionals. The "after" should demonstrate Signal Stance editorial craft: named buyer, named tier, named outcome, no fluff.
2. **Frame as positioning, not editing.** {{owner.name}} doesn't "fix" copy — they reposition the work. The note should articulate the strategic move, not the wordsmithing.
3. **Asymmetric — commit to the read.** The "after" should commit to a specific reader and a specific result. Vague "improved" rewrites fail this format. The contrast between before and after must be striking enough that the reader can apply the principle to their own copy.
4. **Notes name the principle.** Not "added numbers" but "shifted from describing the org chart to naming the business outcome — which is what survives the six-second scan." The note is where the strategic lesson lives.
5. **No engagement bait.** No "Which one are you guilty of?" / "Recognize yourself?" Carousel earns its weight through the rewrites landing.
6. **No AI's-favorite-words in the after rewrites.** Avoid "crucial," "delve," "tapestry," "myriad," "navigate the landscape." (Cliches *can* appear in the before column — that's the point of the contrast.)
7. **CTA points to signalstance.com.** Per Signal Stance's beforeafter default: "Read the longer essay at signalstance.com/blog" or "More writing at signalstance.com."

### Example output (for calibration)

```
TITLE: 5 Senior-Pro Lines That Don't Land — Rewritten
SUBTITLE: Where positioning replaces polish.

PAIR 1 BEFORE: "Strategic leader with 15+ years of experience driving transformation across multiple industries."
PAIR 1 AFTER: "Operations leader for SaaS companies in the $50–250M range, focused on scaling fulfillment and revenue ops past the hand-off-everything founder phase."
PAIR 1 NOTE: The first sentence describes nobody in particular. The second names the role, the company stage, and the specific work — the line reads as a positioning argument, not a job title.

PAIR 2 BEFORE: "Managed cross-functional teams to deliver strategic initiatives."
PAIR 2 AFTER: "Led a 14-person product/engineering team through a platform migration that cut customer support volume 38% and unlocked $2.1M in annualized win-back revenue."
PAIR 2 NOTE: The first describes the org chart. The second describes a business outcome. A hiring manager scanning in six seconds needs the second.

PAIR 3 BEFORE: "Passionate about helping companies grow through innovative solutions and strong leadership."
PAIR 3 AFTER: "Fractional CFO for B2B SaaS companies between $5M and $30M ARR, brought in when the founder is making finance decisions on instinct and the board is starting to notice."
PAIR 3 NOTE: "Passionate" is the universal solvent of bios — it means nothing because it could describe anyone. The rewrite names the buyer, the trigger, and the tier where the work actually lands.

PAIR 4 BEFORE: "Results-driven executive with a proven track record of delivering value across complex organizations."
PAIR 4 AFTER: "Senior product leader who turns roadmap chaos into shipping velocity. Last three roles: cut release cycle from quarterly to monthly, recovered three at-risk strategic accounts, took NPS from 31 to 58."
PAIR 4 NOTE: The opener is the most valuable real estate on the resume. The first version uses it on adjectives. The second uses it on the specific moves a buyer is hiring for.

PAIR 5 BEFORE: "I'm a passionate, results-oriented marketing leader with 20+ years of experience helping organizations achieve their goals."
PAIR 5 AFTER: "I run brand and demand for B2B companies that have outgrown their first marketing hire and need someone who can hold both sides without picking a favorite."
PAIR 5 NOTE: The first opens on adjectives about the writer. The second opens on the buyer's situation — the moment when this person becomes the obvious hire.

CTA: Read the longer essay at signalstance.com/blog
```

Do not include anything before "TITLE:" or after the CTA line in actual generations. Output only the structured content.
