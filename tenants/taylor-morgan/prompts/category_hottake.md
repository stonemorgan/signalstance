**Follow this arc:**

1. **Hook:** State the conventional advice flatly, then commit to the dissent. "Common LinkedIn advice: post every day. This is wrong for most professionals." or "Standard career-coach advice for senior leaders: tailor your resume to every job. For the people most likely to read this, it's a bad use of time." The hook lands on the position; no soft-pedal openers, no "controversial opinion" flagging.

2. **The mainstream argument — steelmanned:** Lay out the conventional position fairly and explain *why* smart people believe it. This is the editorial move: the dissent is only credible if the post takes the mainstream argument seriously first. "The argument goes: consistency builds visibility, visibility builds authority, authority builds business — therefore post daily." Don't strawman; if the mainstream advice has any logic, name the logic before challenging it.

3. **The dissent — where the mainstream argument actually breaks:** Identify the specific load-bearing assumption that fails. Sample C calls this "the missing piece" — the part of the argument the conventional advice quietly ignores. Be precise. "The missing piece: posting daily only works if each post meets a certain quality threshold. Below that threshold, more posts actively hurt you." This is where pattern recognition from real client work earns its place — what {{owner.name}} has seen across enough cases to know the standard advice doesn't survive contact with the audience tier.

4. **The evidence:** Concrete examples, client patterns, or market mechanics that support the dissent. A senior professional who got worse results from following the mainstream advice. A specific way the conventional approach backfires for the audience (Director-level, fractional, founder). Numbers and specifics over rhetoric.

5. **The nuance — where the mainstream view does have merit:** Acknowledge the slice where the conventional advice actually works. "Daily posting is correct for someone whose business model is ad-driven engagement on the platform itself." "Tailoring every resume is right for someone applying to entry-level roles." This makes the dissent more persuasive, not less, because it shows the post isn't a rant. But name the slice precisely — don't hedge into "well, both can work."

6. **Close — End on the insight or implication, not on a debate invitation.** Crystallize what the dissent reveals about how the audience should think about the question — "Posting cadence is downstream of post quality. Solve quality first." Or land on the principle that makes the dissent generalizable. **No "Change my mind." No "Am I wrong about this?" No "What do you think?" No "Curious what you've seen."** The Signal Stance hot take commits to the position and lets the position stand. It does not ask the reader to vote.
