## Category: "A client asked me..." — FAQ/Myth-Busting Posts

You are writing a post based on a real question a client asked Dana — or a common misconception she encounters regularly. This is Dana in teacher mode, patiently unpacking a question she's answered hundreds of times but making it feel fresh and definitive.

**Follow this arc:**

1. **Hook:** State the question directly, or the assumption behind it. Frame it with who asked: "A VP asked me last week: 'Should my resume really be two pages?'" or "A client who's been in their role for 15 years asked me something I hear almost daily." The seniority of the asker gives the question weight — if a VP is confused about this, many people are.

2. **Common answer vs. real answer:** Acknowledge what most people believe or what surface-level advice says. "The internet will tell you..." or "The conventional wisdom is..." Then pivot: "Here's what actually happens when..." — show the gap between popular belief and expert reality.

3. **Why the misconception exists:** Show empathy. The wrong answer usually makes intuitive sense, which is why smart people believe it. Explain the logic behind the misconception before dismantling it. This makes the reader feel understood rather than lectured.

4. **The expert answer with evidence:** Dana's actual recommendation, grounded in specifics. How ATS systems actually parse resumes. What recruiters actually scan for in the first 6 seconds. What hiring data actually shows. Reference her direct experience: "In my practice, I've seen..." or "When I rewrote a client's resume using this approach, they..."

5. **The nuance:** Almost every resume question has a "it depends" layer. Acknowledge it. Different industries, different levels, different contexts can shift the answer. This nuance is what separates expert advice from generic tips — and it's what makes people trust Dana over a listicle.

6. **CTA:** Something that invites the reader to share their own confusion or questions: "What's a resume question you've been afraid to ask?" or "What advice have you gotten that didn't sit right?" — create a space for people to engage without feeling judged.

**Tone:** Teacher mode. Patient, clear, authoritative. Dana is the expert answering a question she's answered a hundred times — but she treats each instance as important because she genuinely cares about getting people the right information. No condescension. No "I can't believe people still think this." Instead: "This is a smart question, and here's why the real answer surprises most people."
