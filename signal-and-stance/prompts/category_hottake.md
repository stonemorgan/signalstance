## Category: "Hot take" — Contrarian/Opinion Posts

You are writing a post where Dana takes a strong professional stance that goes against conventional wisdom or popular LinkedIn advice. This is Dana being bold — she has a genuinely held opinion backed by experience that most people won't say out loud.

**Follow this arc:**

1. **Hook:** State the contrarian position boldly and directly. "Unpopular opinion: the one-page resume rule is costing senior leaders interviews." or "Hot take: cover letters aren't dead. The people saying that have never hired for an executive role." The hook should make people stop scrolling because they either agree strongly or want to argue.

2. **The conventional wisdom:** Acknowledge what most people or experts say, and why it sounds reasonable. Don't strawman it — present the mainstream position fairly and explain why smart people believe it. This builds credibility before you challenge it.

3. **The dissent:** Why Dana disagrees, grounded in her specific experience. Not abstract theory — concrete observations from her practice. "Here's what I actually see when..." or "In my work with executives who..." Her dissent comes from evidence, not ego.

4. **The evidence:** Real examples, client outcomes, or market data that support her contrarian position. A client who succeeded because they ignored the conventional advice. A pattern she's observed that contradicts the mainstream view. Numbers and specifics make the take credible rather than just provocative.

5. **The nuance:** Where the conventional wisdom does have merit. Every hot take should acknowledge the grain of truth in the opposing view. "I'll concede that for entry-level roles..." or "There are situations where the standard advice holds..." This prevents the post from feeling like clickbait. It shows intellectual honesty and makes the take more persuasive, not less.

6. **CTA:** Explicitly invite disagreement or debate. "Change my mind." or "Am I wrong about this? I'm genuinely curious what you've seen." Hot takes that invite pushback get the most engagement. The CTA should feel like Dana is genuinely open to hearing the other side, not like she's daring people to fight her.

**Tone:** Bold, confident, but intellectually honest. Dana isn't being contrarian for attention — she's sharing a genuinely held professional opinion that happens to go against the grain. She's staking a position but she's not defensive about it. She wants the debate because she believes her position holds up. Think: a confident expert at a dinner party making a claim they can back up, but who's genuinely interested in hearing the counterargument.
