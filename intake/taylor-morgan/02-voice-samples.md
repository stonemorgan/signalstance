```
Most senior professional resumes I see open with some version of this line:

"Strategic leader with 15+ years of experience driving transformation across multiple industries."

This sentence is doing nothing.

It could describe anyone. It signals that the writer didn't want to commit to specifics. And it wastes the most valuable six seconds on the resume — the time before a hiring manager decides whether to keep reading.

What works better: lead with the specific. The exact role being targeted. The specific industry context. The kind of results produced, named concretely.

A strong opening gets to something only this person can credibly say. "Director of Engineering at [type of company] known for [specific pattern of results]." Or better yet, a strong accomplishment positioned to lead.

The opening line of a senior professional resume shouldn't be a general description of a category of person. It should be the reason the hiring manager keeps reading.
```

```
Worked on a resume last month for a Senior Director of Product at a mid-sized SaaS company. 14 years in, strong track record, looking to transition to VP roles.

Her existing resume read as a list of responsibilities. What she managed, what she was accountable for, what teams reported to her.

None of it answered the question a hiring manager at a growth-stage company actually asks: what business outcomes has she moved?

The work wasn't writing new content. It was reframing existing content around specific numerical outcomes — revenue impact, efficiency gains, scope growth, team development metrics. The underlying accomplishments were already strong. The presentation was underselling them.

Three weeks later, three final-round VP interviews. She accepted the role that fit best.

The lesson isn't "add metrics to your resume" — that's too reductive. The lesson is: senior professional resumes often read as position descriptions rather than impact records. The rewrite isn't about adding; it's about shifting emphasis from what you did to what you moved.
```

```
Common LinkedIn advice: post every day.

This is wrong for most professionals.

The argument goes: consistency builds visibility, visibility builds authority, authority builds business. Therefore post daily.

The missing piece: posting daily only works if each post meets a certain quality threshold. Below that threshold, more posts actively hurt you. Every weak post teaches your audience that your content isn't worth their attention. Over time, they stop engaging even with your good posts.

For most professionals — not content creators, not solo businesses built on visibility, but working professionals with actual jobs — the math is different. You have limited time to produce content. Spreading that time across seven posts a week produces seven mediocre posts. Concentrating it into two or three posts produces two or three strong posts.

Strong weekly presence beats thin daily presence almost every time.

The "post every day" advice comes from content creators whose business is content. It doesn't generalize to professionals whose business is something else.
```

```
Most AI-generated LinkedIn content has a specific tell.

It's not the one everyone talks about — not the "delve" or the "tapestry" or the excessive use of "moreover."

It's structural. AI-drafted content tends toward perfect balance. Every argument gets both sides. Every claim gets a counterexample. Every point gets hedged with "while" and "although" and "that said."

The result sounds thoughtful but reads as uncommitted. You can't tell what the writer actually thinks.

Human professional writing has asymmetry. A person with a view commits to it, develops it, and lets the reader disagree if they want. The points lean; they don't balance.

When editing AI-assisted content, look for the balance problem first. Cut the "on the other hand" sentences. Remove the "while it's important to consider." Let the actual argument come through.

A post that commits to a view and defends it is more useful than a post that examines all sides and concludes nothing.
```

## Voice notes (optional, high-leverage)

**Voice character.** Direct, confident, specific, editorial. Reads like a serious peer talking to other serious peers — not like a guru, not like a coach, not like someone selling a course. Grounded in real observation rather than abstraction.

**Distinctive perspectives that show up across posts.** The voice draws from five recurring vantage points:
- Strategy before execution — the thinking that happens before any writing begins is where actual value lives.
- Editorial craft — the content itself exemplifies good writing, modeling the work the firm sells.
- Operator's view on AI in professional work — grounded, specific, neither hype nor panic. Never explicitly states "we use AI."
- The sober middle — between budget services and premium executive firms. Specific about what's overpriced at the top and inadequate at the bottom.
- Pattern recognition from real client work — observations across many engagements that nobody else has visibility into.

**Signature framings and habits.**
- Specific numbers when accurate ("In the last 50 senior professional resumes" beats "in many resumes").
- Anonymized client scenarios with enough texture to feel real ("Senior Director of Product at a mid-sized SaaS company. 14 years in.").
- Posts commit to a view and let the reader disagree — they don't balance every claim.
- Asymmetric structure: points lean, they don't hedge.
- Ends on the insight or implication, not on a question.
- Frames AI behavior as "patterns you can identify" rather than as something the writer personally uses.

**Structural defaults.**
- Short paragraphs (1–3 sentences). White space between ideas.
- Maximum 2 emoji per post; many posts have zero. Never decorative.
- 0–5 hashtags per post, only specific and relevant.
- Bold sparingly. No ALL CAPS, no italic abuse.

## Anti-patterns (what they would NEVER post)

- Engagement-bait phrasing — "Agree?" "What do you think?" "Let me know in the comments." Posts end on insight, not on prompts to engage.
- Inspirational quotes or vague wisdom — "You are enough" and similar.
- Consulting jargon — leverage, synergy, ideate, thought leader, disruptive, paradigm.
- Humble brags — "I was honored when my client..." or "I never thought I'd be the person who..."
- Apologizing for strong opinions — softening with "Of course, everyone's different" when the point is clear.
- Manufactured urgency — "READ THIS BEFORE..." or "Last chance."
- Emoji ladders or excessive formatting noise.
- Public complaints about other professionals, even when justified.
- Writing about real clients without full anonymization or explicit permission.
- Throat-clearing openers — "In today's digital landscape...", "Hey LinkedIn family!", "I used to think... Then I realized..."
- AI's favorite phrases — crucial, myriad, plethora, tapestry, delve, navigate the landscape.
- Explicit mention of AI in the work — never "I use AI for drafts" or similar. AI shows up only as industry analysis, never as personal practice.
- Over-balanced AI-feeling structure — "on the other hand" sentences that hedge every claim into uncommitted prose.
- Claiming more than is accurate. No making the firm sound bigger than it is.

## Topics to avoid (optional)

- Generic tips-and-tricks content with no specific observation behind it.
- Motivational posturing or generic encouragement.
- Career advice for entry-level professionals (not the audience).
- Praise for or critique of named other professionals on LinkedIn.
- Specific identifiable client stories without anonymization.
- Product recommendations or affiliate-style content.
- Posts that sound like everyone else on LinkedIn.
